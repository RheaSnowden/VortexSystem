# Proof-of-Concept Variant Using Proven Technology

**Status**: Conceptual comparison — shows a near-term, buildable alternative using existing, commercially available technology.

## Purpose
This page presents a **proof-of-concept (POC) version** of the VORTEX tunnel system that relies on proven, off-the-shelf components instead of the advanced (but untested) hydro and steam sleeves. The goal is to demonstrate that the core tunnel architecture — thermosiphon-driven dual-phase flow, continuous operation, modular maintenance, and combined hydro + steam energy harvesting — can be realized with current technology while providing a realistic baseline for comparison against the higher-performance sleeve concept.

## Approach
- **Hydro side**: Use proven watermill or in-pipe hydro turbine technology (e.g., modern waterwheels, LucidPipe-style turbines, or pump-as-turbine units) linked to a shared driveshaft. Canals can be shaped and optimized for these devices.
- **Steam side**: Use conventional low-pressure steam turbines or expanders (existing industrial technology).
- **Mutual rotation concept**: Water flows downward through the hydro units while steam rises and drives the steam turbines from the lower side, creating mutual clockwise rotation on the shared shaft.
- **Overall layout**: Same inclined tunnel (30° preferred), stepped basins, thermal fluid cascade, and continuous-running design as the main concept. The only major change is replacing the hypothetical sleeves with proven rotating machinery.

This version sacrifices some potential efficiency for immediate buildability and testability.

## Comparison: POC Variant vs. Advanced Sleeve Concept

| Aspect                        | POC Variant (Proven Tech)                  | Advanced Sleeve Concept                  | Notes |
|-------------------------------|--------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|-------|
| Hydro harvesting              | Watermills / in-pipe turbines (proven)     | Custom hydro sleeves (untested)          | POC is immediately testable |
| Steam harvesting              | Conventional low-pressure steam turbines   | Custom steam sleeves (untested)          | Both viable; sleeves projected higher performance |
| Expected efficiency (hydro)   | 35–65%                                     | 50–70% (target)                          | Real-world POC numbers are known |
| Expected efficiency (steam)   | 30–50%                                     | 40–55% (target)                          | Depends on steam conditions |
| Overall system performance    | Lower but achievable                       | Higher projected                         | Sleeves offer upside potential |
| Development risk              | Low                                        | Medium-High                              | POC reduces technical risk |
| Maintenance                   | Standard industrial (nonstop capable)      | Quick-swap modular (nonstop)             | Both support continuous operation |
| Canal / tunnel modifications  | Moderate (optimized for watermills)        | Designed around sleeves                  | Both feasible |
| Best use case                 | Early demonstration & validation           | High-performance future deployments      | Complementary approaches |

## Performance Estimates (Using Plant Bowen Scenario)
The same 400 MWth total captured heat scenario from the [Plant Bowen example](plant-bowen-example.md) is used here for direct comparison.

**POC Variant (Proven Technology)**
- Conservative output: **80–150 MW electric** continuous
- Optimistic output: **150–250 MW electric** continuous
- Annual (85–90% capacity factor): **≈0.6–2.0 TWh/year**
- Per section (150 ft): Roughly 2–4 MW electric (lower than sleeve version due to proven-component limits)

**Advanced Sleeve Version (for reference)**
- Conservative: 200–325 MW electric
- Optimistic: 400–800+ MW electric
- See the [Plant Bowen example](plant-bowen-example.md) for full details and ranges.

**Key takeaway**: The POC version delivers meaningful power and validates the overall tunnel concept today, while the sleeve version represents the higher-performance potential once the technology is developed and tested.

## Benefits of the POC Approach
- Uses commercially available, field-proven equipment.
- Significantly lower technical and financial risk for initial builds.
- Allows real-world testing of the full thermosiphon loop, dual-phase harvesting, continuous operation, and modular maintenance concepts.
- Provides a solid baseline for measuring future improvements from optimized sleeves.
- Faster path to a working demonstration system.

## Limitations
- Lower overall efficiency and power output compared to the advanced sleeve design.
- May require more canal shaping and mechanical integration work for the watermills and shared driveshaft.
- Conventional steam turbines are less optimized for the specific wet/dual-phase conditions inside the tunnel.

## Relationship to the Main VORTEX Concept
The POC variant and the advanced sleeve concept are **complementary**, not competing. The POC version can serve as:
- An early demonstration platform.
- A lower-risk deployment option for certain sites.
- A stepping stone while sleeve technology is refined.

Both approaches share the same core advantages: nonstop operation, modular maintenance, heat saturation benefits from continuous running, and strong potential for multi-source heat integration (including low-pressure steam from plants like Bowen, data centers, solar, and H₂).

## Next Steps for Exploration
- Detailed engineering study of canal modifications for watermill integration.
- Selection of specific commercial watermill / in-pipe turbine models.
- Thermodynamic modeling of the shared-driveshaft mutual rotation concept.
- Pilot-scale test of a short tunnel section using proven components.

This comparison illustrates that multiple viable paths exist. The VORTEX system is presented as a flexible concept family — with both near-term realizable versions and higher-performance future options — all built around the same elegant inclined tunnel architecture.
