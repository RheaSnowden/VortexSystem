
**Hydro Sleeves – Detailed Design & Operation**

The hydro sleeves are the core energy extraction components of the VORTEX system. They convert the kinetic energy of flowing water into rotational mechanical energy, which is then transferred to a driveshaft and generator.

### Overview & Purpose
Hydro sleeves are modular, extractable turbine units installed directly in the main canals. They are designed for distributed, low-head energy harvesting while remaining easy to maintain and replace. The system favors parallel paired sleeves per section for stability, though grouped arrangements (tri/quad) are possible with adjusted canal geometry.

### Design Details
- **Basic form**: Cylindrical sleeve with internal spiral fins or blades optimized for low-to-moderate flow velocities.
- **Typical dimensions** (reference scale): Approximately 1.5–3 m in diameter and 6–8 m long, depending on canal size and desired torque.
- **External cog**: Each sleeve has an external cog or drive interface on the dry side for power transfer.
- **Magnetic coupling system**:
  - **Stabilizing ring (rear)**: The canal’s full-pipe section fits partially into the sleeve. A SmCo ring on the pipe faces outward and overlaps with a corresponding inward-facing ring on the sleeve. This provides secure, low-maintenance alignment and stability during operation.
  - **Front magnetic ring**: A separate SmCo ring at the forward end holds the sleeve in place. This ring can be lowered or disengaged to allow extraction.
- **Extractability**: The sleeve is designed for quick removal. The front ring is lowered, the sleeve is decoupled from the cog (if present) and any additional stabilizers, and then pulled forward off the overlapping rear rings along the guide system. Replacement is the reverse process.

### Flow Interaction & Anticipated Effects
- **Pre-rotation**: The transition zone before the sleeve includes internal grooves, fins, or rifling to impart initial rotation to the water. This improves sleeve efficiency and reduces entry turbulence.
- **Torque generation**: Water flowing through the sleeve interacts with the spiral fins, producing rotational force on the sleeve.
- **Impact cushioning**: The post-sleeve canal is widened and the overflow returns water to soften the high-velocity exit spray, reducing erosion on downstream surfaces.
- **Self-cleaning tendency**: Continuous flow and rotation help limit fouling compared to static surfaces.

### Options & Variants
- **Sleeve grouping**: Paired sleeves (baseline) for stability; tri or quad configurations possible with wider canals and adjusted overflow routing.
- **Fin geometry**: Looser spiral fins for lower flows; tighter or longer fins for higher torque applications. Fin length and pitch can be optimized for specific flow rates.
- **Coupling methods**: Indirect gearing or gearboxes (recommended for flexibility) or magnetic couplings for zero mechanical penetration.
- **Materials**: Lightweight, high-strength composites or coated metals chosen for corrosion resistance, temperature tolerance, and low weight. The sleeve should be recyclable at end of life.

### Generator Connection Arrangements
- **Indirect coupling (preferred)**: External cogs on the sleeves connect via idler gears or short gearboxes to a main driveshaft running in a dry gallery parallel to the canals.
- **Driveshaft layout**: Can run above, beside, or below the wet zone in a sealed, accessible gallery.
- **Multiple sleeves per shaft**: Typically 2–4 sleeves per driveshaft segment, depending on torque limits and maintenance strategy.
- **Generator placement**: Located in dry rooms accessible from the maintenance gallery. Gearboxes can be used to match sleeve RPM to optimal generator speed.

### Projected Performance
Power output depends heavily on flow rate, head (slope), heat input (for steam side), and overall system optimization.

**Rough scaling example** (illustrative only):
- At moderate flow (~20–30 m³/s total across a multi-canal section) and good sleeve density, expect roughly **50–300+ MW per mile** of effective tunnel length, depending on heat augmentation and optimization.
- Higher performance (approaching or exceeding the upper end) is possible with strong steam/waste heat input and long ringed layouts.

**Force / Torque considerations**:
- Torque is generated primarily in the first half of the sleeve where flow velocity and fin interaction are strongest.
- The second half primarily provides stabilization.
- Exact calculations require CFD modeling and prototype testing. The system is designed to be testable at small scale before full deployment.

All performance numbers should be treated as directional guidance with clear assumptions. Real output will vary significantly based on site-specific conditions.

### Maintenance & Modularity
- Sleeves are designed for quick extraction using rail-guided systems and magnetic decoupling.
- External cogs and gearboxes are accessible from the dry maintenance gallery.
- Modular design allows individual sleeve replacement without affecting neighboring units.

### Gap & Leakage Management
A small gap between the pipe transition and the hydro sleeve is expected. Some water ingress into the mechanical gallery is likely, particularly from sleeve exit spray.

**Recommended handling**:
- Intentional collection channels or gutters catch leakage early and reroute it (ideally before reaching the cog/gear connection) back into the lower main canal or a stepped basin.
- Drainage paths and sloped gallery flooring direct any residual water away from sensitive mechanical and electrical components.
- This keeps heat and water exposure in the lower maintenance area to a minimum. Note that the temperature gradient in the tunnel (hotter upper sections near steam, boiling near stepped basins, cooler lower areas) should be considered for insulation and material choices in the gallery.

### Reversibility (Pump / Circulator Mode)
The sleeve concept can be reversed: driving the external cog turns the sleeve into an inline pump or circulator. This enables gentle fluid movement or mixing in low-head applications and provides operational flexibility (e.g., circulation during low-flow periods).

### Integration with Canal & Basin System
- Sleeves sit in the main canals after the pre-rotation transition zone.
- Post-sleeve widening and overflow return cushion the exit flow.
- Stepped basins provide additional flow control and injection points around sleeve sections.

### Considerations & Limitations
- Performance is sensitive to flow velocity and consistency.
- Magnetic coupling strength and material durability at operating temperatures should be validated.
- Overload protection (magnetic slip or mechanical shear) is recommended.
- Initial testing and CFD modeling are strongly advised before large-scale deployment.

The hydro sleeve is the primary mechanical heart of the VORTEX system — a modular, maintainable, and potentially reversible component that turns distributed low-head flow into usable rotational energy.
